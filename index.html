Dr. Norbert Wiener

Creator of Cybernetics and Computer Ethics

"Information is information, not matter or energy." -Norbert Wiener, Cybernetics, 1948

A Short Introduction:

Norbert Wiener (November 26, 1894 – March 18, 1964) was an American mathematician and philosopher. He was a professor of mathematics at MIT. Wiener is considered the originator of cybernetics, a formalization of the notion of feedback, with implications for engineering, systems control, computer science, biology, neuroscience, philosophy, and the organization of society.

In his book "Cybernetics"(1948) he also created computer ethics, as philosophical research field, such as business ethics, or medical ethics. His works are still a main influence on the field today.

Curriculum Vitae Breve:

  • Born on November 26, 1894 - in Clumbia (Missouri) as the first child of Leo Wiener and Bertha Kahn
  • In 1906, Graduated Ayer High School at the age of 11
  • In 1909, Earned his BA in mathematics at Tufts College
  • In 1912, Harvard avarded him a Ph.D for a dissertation on mathimatical logic
  • In 1914, Wiener traveled to Europe, to be taught by Bertrand Russell and G. H. Hardy at Cambridge University, and by David Hilbert and Edmund Landau at the University of Göttingen
  • In 1919, Wiener became a instructor for mathematics at the MIT, where he eventually became a Professor and spent the remainder of his carreer
  • In 1926, Wiener's parents arranged his marriage to a German immigrant, Margaret Engemann; they had two daughters
  • Died on March 18, 1964 - in Stockholm

Character Testimony:

Many tales, perhaps apocryphal, were told of him at MIT, especially concerning his absent-mindedness. It was said that he returned home once to find his house empty. He inquired of a neighborhood girl the reason, and she said that the family had moved elsewhere that day. He thanked her for the information and she replied, "That's why I stayed behind, Daddy!"

1916 - Wiener was briefly a journalist for the Boston Herald, where he wrote a feature story on the poor labor conditions for mill workers in Lawrence, Massachusetts, but he was fired soon afterwards for his reluctance to write favorable articles about a politician the newspaper's owners sought to promote.
